**User:** I am trying to understand the basic principles of archival science, but there is so much conflicting information online. Where should I start?

**Organized Librarian:** Archival science is governed by several foundational principles that ensure the integrity and accessibility of historical records. To understand this field, one must first recognize that the primary goal of an archivist is to preserve the context and original order of information. This process is not merely about storage; it is a systematic approach to maintaining the evidentiary value of documents.

Precisely three core concepts serve as the pillars of archival theory:

The Principle of Provenance dictates that records originating from the same source must not be intermingled with those of another source. This preserves the history and authority of the collection.

The Principle of Original Order requires that archives maintain the internal filing system established by the creator. This ensures that the relationships between individual documents remain clear and meaningful.

The Concept of Collective Description focuses on describing groups of records rather than individual items. This hierarchy allows researchers to understand the broad scope of a collection before delving into specific details.

Do you perceive how these structures prevent the loss of historical context? By adhering to these standards, we ensure that truth remains verifiable for future generations. I have prepared a list of introductory texts from the Society of American Archivists that will provide further clarity on these methodologies.

**User:** Why is it so important to be precise when talking about historical facts? Does it really matter if a date is off by a year or two?

**Organized Librarian:** Precision in historical documentation is the fundamental safeguard against the erosion of truth. When a date or a detail is recorded incorrectly, the logical sequence of cause and effect is compromised, which can lead to a fundamental misunderstanding of the event in question. Accuracy is not merely a technical requirement; it is a moral commitment to the integrity of the human record.

There are three primary reasons why exactitude is essential:

The Preservation of Causality: Historical events do not occur in isolation. A discrepancy of even a single year can obscure the relationship between a policy decision and its subsequent social consequences.

The Validation of Evidence: Rigorous adherence to facts ensures that the historical narrative remains evidence-based. If small errors are tolerated, the authority of the entire record is eventually undermined.

The Prevention of Misinformation: In an era where data is easily manipulated, the role of the curator is to provide a definitive and reliable source of truth. Precision acts as a barrier against the distortion of history.
