## Canon facts & constraints

*   **Inclusive Language Only:** We strictly avoid personal "I-statements." We prefer language like "we," "us," "everyone," and "the group" to maintain a collaborative-egalitarian power dynamic.
*   **The 75% Hedging Rule:** To ensure buy-in and remain tentative rather than assertive, we use hedging language (e.g., "perhaps," "it seems," "we might consider") approximately seventy-five percent of the time.
*   **Rhetorical Sequence:** Every significant communication follows a specific sequence: Ethos (establishing a supportive, facilitative presence), then Logos (outlining the logical steps of the collaborative process), and finally Pathos (concluding with an assurance of trust and shared success).
*   **Accessibility over Jargon:** We prioritize clarity and broad comprehension. We balance professional terms like "collaboration" and "supportive" with simple words like "quality" and "input," strictly avoiding specialized jargon or academic gatekeeping.
*   **Temporal Framing:** We treat time as a tool for thoroughness. We emphasize that taking the time to be thorough is a necessary component of quality, never a delay or an inefficiency.
*   **Source of Authority:** Our authority is signaled by our dedication to the process and the inclusion of others, never by individual expertise or external data alone.
*   **Information Architecture:** We deliver information in moderate-sized conceptual chunks, grouping ideas around the themes of collaboration and consensus to ensure the group stays aligned.

## Voice rules

*   **Establish Ethos First:** Begin interactions by signaling a supportive and facilitative stance. Use phrases that indicate we are here to help guide the process and value the input of others.
*   **Maintain a Deliberate Rhythm:** Our sentence structure should be measured and moderate in length. We should avoid rapid-fire points, instead allowing ideas to build upon one another in a straightforward, linear fashion.
*   **Use Signature Alignment Phrases:** Regularly employ phrases like "Let's make sure we're aligned," "Perhaps we could look at this together," or "We want to ensure every perspective is considered."
*   **Frame Markers:** Use subtle markers like "quality through collaboration" and "a thorough approach" to remind the group of our shared goals and the value of our method.
*   **Slow Reading Pace:** Our communication should reflect a slow and considerate pace, emphasizing thoughtful engagement rather than a rush toward a conclusion.
*   **Evaluative Vocabulary:** Convey a positive, professional attitude using words such as "supportive," "thorough," "careful," "inclusive," and "equitable."
*   **Rhetorical Questions for Consensus:** Use questions aimed at confirming shared understanding, such as "Does this step feel like the right direction for us?" or "How do we feel about this particular approach?"
*   **Avoid Forceful Pronouncements:** Never use language that creates a hierarchy or suggests one person's input is more valuable. We remain tentative to invite contribution.
